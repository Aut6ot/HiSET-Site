Overview

In this decade, manned exploration is briefly covered, which is mainly just about the start of the Space Shuttle program. Then we move on to the Robotic Mission, of which I have covered 8.

MANNED ACTIVITY

SALYUT

Salyut-7 was the last of the Salyut series, and it was a space station. It, along with its predecessors, paved the way for futher advancement, and the next level-up: Mir.

SPACE SHUTTLE

NASA's Space Shuttle program begins in this decade. Many successful flights occured, and led to lots of firsts in terms of Astronauts, including the first American woman in space, Sally Ride.

Among all these successes, there was one very serious failure, with the Challenger accident resulting in the deaths of all 7 astronauts aboard.

MIR

Mir was a Soviet, then later Russian, space station that orbited the Earth. It was expanded upon over time, and served as a center to conduct space research.

ROBOTIC MISSIONS

GALILEO

SPACECRAFT

SLV: SHUTTLE ATLANTIS

Galileo was an orbiter-type spacecraft sent to Jupiter at the very tail-end of the decade in 1989. It wouldn't actually arrive at the planet until the middle of the next decade. Galileo far outlasted its original mission, studying Jupiter and its moons, and continued to operate until 2003 where it was driven to fall into Jupiter.

I got to see a full-scale model of this at the Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL)'s museum, and this thing is/was huge. Truly. And I learned that the umbrella-looking thing at the top (in the picture, it's to the left)- a high-gain antenna- did not properly extend all the way, and yet scientists and engineers were able to work around it and still communicate using the low-gain antennas.

TARGET: JUPITER

DEVELOPERS

NASA

MAGELLAN

SPACECRAFT

SLV: SHUTTLE ATLANTIS

Magellan was a spacecraft that was sent to study Venus and notably mapped out the entire surface. This was done using radar, since Venus is covered in clouds.

Its major achievements include that it was the first deep-space craft to be deployed by a Space Shuttle, and it was the first spacecraft to fully map out Venus.

A lot of the computer-generated images of Venus's surface that you can find come from this craft. Pretty neat!

TARGET: VENUS

SAKIGAKE

SPACECRAFT

SLV: M-3SII

This was Japan's first deep-space craft, intended to be a practice fly-by for their main Halley's Comet spacecraft, Suisei. Though the latter spacecraft was not successful, Sakigake itself was a major first for Japan, and was able to serve as a reference point for ESA's Giotto as it approached Halley's Comet.

TARGET: HALLEY'S COMET

DEVELOPERS

ISAS - Institute of Space and Astronautical Science (Japan)

VEGA

SPACECRAFT

SLV: PROTON-K

The project VeGa was named for its two targets, Venus & Halley's Comet (named Gallei in Russian). The project consisted of two main identical spacecraft: Vega 1 and Vega 2. Both had probes and landers they were carrying that they basically just dropped off at Venus, then set course for Halley's Comet. At the comet, they both took measurements of the dust.

I did get to see a model one of the Vegas earlier this year, though I didn't really understand the significance of it at the time. It's pretty cool, pretty big and funky-looking, and is at the Udvar-Hazy Smithsonian Air & Space museum in Virginia. Although it's not the real thing, as both will forever be in a heliocentric orbit.

TARGET: VENUS & HALLEY'S COMET

DEVELOPERS

USSR

COBE

SPACECRAFT

SLV: DELTA 5000

COBE, also known as Explorer 66, stands for the Cosmic Microwave Background Explorer. Developed by NASA and put into Earth's orbit, it observed the oldest light that exists in the universe (and comes from the Big Bang), the Cosmic Microwave Background. Doing so provided a lot of insight and changed understanding of how the universe might have formed.

TARGET: COSMIC MICROWAVE BACKGROUND

DEVELOPERS

NASA

HIPPARCOS

SPACECRAFT

SLV: ARIANE-4

Another ESA mission, this telescope aimed to map the universe, its stars, and their movements. It's a predecessor to the Gaia mission, which will come up in a later decade.

TARGET: UNIVERSE

DEVELOPERS

ESA

IRAS

SPACECRAFT

SLV: DELTA 3910

IRAS, standing for Infrared Astronomical Satellite, was an international effort to observe and catalog the solar system. It was the first space telescope to observe in infrared.

A pretty cool achievement of this one is that it was able to observe much deeper into the core of the Milky Way than any previous telescope.
