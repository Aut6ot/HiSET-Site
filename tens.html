SUN

PARKER SOLAR PROBE

Though there have been many spacecraft pointed at the Sun to study it, none of them are quite like the Parker Solar Probe. This probe is, quote, "on a mission to touch the Sun"(from the NASA page on it). Launched in 2018, it is still very much active and transmitting back data. At its closest approach to the Sun, it travels at an absolutely insane 430,000 mph.

Fun fact: my JPL contact (yes I keep bringing this up because I want to brag) shared that the project used to be called "Solar Probe Plus", or "SPP", though this abbreviation sounds really funny to children, so that's possibly part of the reason they renamed it.

MERCURY

BEPICOLOMBO

BepiColombo is a joint mission between ESA and JAXA (Japan), with each having their own orbiter along for the ride. It launched in 2018, yet its mission is still not completed. It is planned to arrive at Mercury about a year from me writing this. And this whole mission is a wild undertaking because, according to ESA, it's easier to travel to Saturn than it is to reach Mercury.

VENUS

あかつき

AKATSUKI

Akatsuki was a mission by ISAS/JAXA and studied Venus's atmosphere. It complemented ESA's Venus Express, both aimed at studying the same target. Something impressive about this mission is that it failed the first time while trying to insert into the planet's orbit in 2010. It wouldn't be until 2015 where the spacecraft successfully entered orbit, and was able to begin its science measurements.

MOON

嫦娥

CHANG'E

The Chang'e Lunar Exploration series of Chinese lunar landers continued on from the previous decade. In this decade, the ones launched include Chang'e 2, 3, 4, and 5-T1. All were successful. 5-T1 was a test run for Chang'e 5, which launched in the next decade. Numbers 3 and 4 launched with Yutu-1 and Yutu-2, respectively, which were lunar rovers. They're actually quite cute if you look them up. Yutu-2 is still active today, and continues to explore the far side of the Moon (the first lunar rover ever to do so!).

GRAIL

NASA sure does love its acronyms. This one stands for "Gravity Recovery And Interior Laboratory". The project consisted of two twin spacecraft, Ebb and Flow. Sent to the Moon, they worked together to map the lunar gravitational field. They are currently collecting dust on the moon because their mission ended and they were intentionally crashed.

LADEE

Another NASA one- "Lunar Atmosphere and Dust Environment Explorer". This orbiter studied the Moon's surface and atmosphere ("exosphere", really) for 6 months before being intentionally crashed on the surface (I find it funny that from the NASA page, they make it very clear and state multiple times that it was intentional. So don't go getting the wrong idea). Major discoveries of this mission included: better understandings of lunar water and lunar dust, and the gases that make up the lunar exosphere.

MARS

CURIOSITY

Also called the Mars Science Laboratory, this rover was sent to Mars in 2011 and arrived the next year. It went with a focus on determining if the planet was capable of supporting life in the past. The rover posesses a whole host of various science instruments that would be too much to list here. And it's still active today! You can even see where it's currently located on its mission page.

INSIGHT

You can call it "Interior Exploration using Seismic Investigations, Geodesy and Heat Transport", or you can call it InSight. This Martian lander launched and arrived at the planet in 2018, with a focus on studying the interior structure of Mars. Its design was based off the Mars Phoenix lander. It concluded its mission just a few years ago in 2022.

MANGALYAAN

Mangalyaan was ISRO (India)'s first mission to Mars, and it was a success, making India the 4th nation to successful orbit a spacecraft around Mars. It's also abbreviated as MOM for Mars Orbiter Mission. It tested various scientific measuring systems, to pave the way for future Martian missions. It lasted far beyond its 6-month mission which started in 2013, and ended in 2021.

MAVEN

MAVEN, short for "Mars Atmosphere and Volatile EvolutioN", was a NASA orbiter launched to Mars in 2013 and arrived in 2014. Its purpose was to explore and understand the Martian upper atmosphere. The craft is still active today, and is one of the 5 orbiters that make up and support the Mars Relay Network.

ASTEROIDS

HAYABUSA

This is Hayabusa-2, to be exact. Developed by JAXA, it was sent on a 6-year mission to an asteroid called Ryugu. It collected samples from the asteroid and successfully brought them back to Earth in December 2020 (what a time). It's since been given an extended mission and is currently travelling to another smaller asteroid.

OSIRIS-REX

Asteroid missions have been mentioned already, but this was NASA's first sample-collecting one, and it was focused on a particular asteroid called Bennu. Its name is short for "Origins, Spectral Interpretation, Resource Identification, and Security – Regolith Explorer". It's quite similar to Hayabusa-2, in that it successfully collected samples, dropped it off back to Earth, and has been given an extended misison to another asteroid Apophis. It's expected to arrive in 2029.

JUPITER

JUNO

No acronym this time :) Juno is simply named after a Roman goddess, who was Jupiter's wife and queen of the gods. It's a NASA probe/orbiter focused on exploring Jupiter and some of its moons. Launched in 2011, it travelled for 5 years and 1.7 billion miles before finally arriving at the Jovian system. It has been responsible for many of the pretty high-quality images that we currently have of Jupiter and its moons. It's still active on an extended mission today.

ETC. MISSIONS

GAIA

Remember ESA's Hipparcos from the 80s? Gaia is the successor to it. Launched in 2014, it's worked to create a 3D map of the Milky Way. It has made over 3 trillion observations of 2 billion stars and objects. That's a lot of data, and it's just for one galaxy. The spacecraft concluded its observational period earlier this year in January, but the project will still be active until possibly beyond 2030, as there are still two more planned data releases.

IKAROS

In all the research I've done for this project, I've never seen a spacecraft that looks like IKAROS. Made by JAXA, it was a technology demonstration of a solar-powered sail satellite. It operated from its launch in 2010 to just this year, ending operations in May 2025.
